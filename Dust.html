1. Dust Bunnies Animation

As a team of five we were tasked to create an 3D animated film. My role on this project was mainly animating, rigging, and some 3d modeling. Dust Bunnies is an animated short about a lonesome dust bunny named Dustin, who lives in a corner of an apartment. He dreams of being vacuumed by the apartment's vacuum cleaner, Roomba, however, due to a thumbtack being stuck on Dustin’s head, Roomba refuses to vacuum him out of his corner. It is not until later when the apartment owner picks up the thumbtack along with Dustin, freeing him from his corner and tossing him through the air. Escaped from the corner, Dustin lands in Roomba’s direct path and is finally vacuumed, joining his fellow dust bunnies at the nightclub located inside Roomba.

2. StoryBoard

After brainstorming and narrowing down our ideas we decided to storyboard out our film. This is a series of sketches showing different shots and narrative beats of the film. This step helped us divide works among each other. For example, Andy will animate scenes 1-13 and so on. Here I found that my lack of drawing skills did not end up effect my team because we all ended up animating our own parts. Lastly, we put together a video with narrations of the script and each scene of the storyboard being shown.

4. Project Implementation Process

After brainstorming and writing our script and storyboard, our team listed out all assets that were required to be modeled and textured, assigning each member with at least one asset to create. After all the modeling and texturing were completed, our team then divided our storyboard into a list of cinematic shots and assigned each member with different shots to animate and render individually. I was tasked with the majority of the animation starting for 0:16 to 1:22 of the film. Furthermore, I was also tasked figuring out the fur texture of the dust.

5. Texturing

I was tasked with figuring out how to texture the fury that would be on the dust bunnies. Which lead to the biggest issue we encountered during this production with xGen, the geometry instancer tool, as fur files were not properly transferring to different scenes on different computers. As we were assigned with different scenes to animate, each member needed to import required models into their working folders. However, when we attempted to import Dustin and other dust bunnies into our scene folders, we found that our xGen fur files would not properly transfer through. As a result, the fur texture did not load into different scenes.


To ensure that our xGen files properly transferred through to different scenes, we made sure to import our bunnies into our Maya files. If importing the file was not successful, we would then go into our xGen file path and manually rewrite the paths to ensure that Maya could locate the xGen files from the proper folder.


Another issue that arose with xGen was that the fur would not connect to our model’s mesh when when animating Dustin. The exact reason behind why the fur would not connect with the mesh is still unclear as not all team members experienced this issue. However, for times when we did encounter this issue, we would either restart Maya or re-import the textured model back into our scenes. If that was not successful, then to ensure that the fur would follow our model’s mesh, we copied the animation keyframes of the skeleton to the fur.


6. Animating

Since I was tasked with a large portion of the animation, I really tried to follow the 12 principle of animation. One of the principles I really followed and tried to portrait in our film was squash and stretch. This is meant to give a sense of weight and flexibility to the object. This was especially important for this project since, there were no dialogue for the character, therefore, it was very important for us to convey emotions through animation. Another way I tired to bring life into our characters is through the principle, slow in and slow out. This was to hopefully show realistic movement between objects. Since, things in real life take time to accelerate and slow down. A lot of this was done in the Maya graph editor which provides curves to show the speed of animation. Using this editor, I was able to follow the 12 principle of animation.

7. Putting it All Together

Once each team member has finished animating their own section, all the scenes were rendered and put together inside of Adobe Premiere. Where sound effects and background music are added. Here is our finished product.

8. Reflection

This project helped me understand the power of new media tools and how it can be combined with traditional direction to create something that is impossible to shoot with a normal camera. The array of camera angles and movements provided by animation tools such as Maya is endless. However, by combining traditional cinematography techniques such as meaning behind each shot I learned to create a meaningful animation.
